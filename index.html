ML-6G: Machine Learning for 6G Wireless Networks

July 2022

The workshop Machine Learning for 6G Wireless Networks (ML-6G) will highlight the critical role of AI/ML technologies in designing the emerging sixth-generation (6G) wireless networking systems. This workshop will present pioneering inter-disciplinary work in this rapidly-evolving area. In particular, it will bring the ML and wireless networking researchers together in order to (i) expose the ICML community to the unique opportunities and challenges of introducingAI/ML in wireless networking systems that distinguish them from other areas in which ML algorithms are prevalent and (ii) carve out strategic research targets for the deployment of AI/ML technologies in 6G wireless networks and beyond.

While 5G is being deployed around the globe, academic and industry researchers are laying out the vision and key technologies for 6G. 6G systems promise huge economic and societal impacts by merging computation, communication, and intelligence in order to create new digital worlds that connect and control both physical and biological systems. Just as 4G enabled unprecedented smartphone services and applications, such as Uber,Google Maps, and Netflix, and 5G is facilitating advancements such as autonomous driving and mixed reality, 6G is envisioned to result in novel application areas that are yet to be created. 5G standards are already addressing the use of ML for the radio access network (RAN) and the air interface, laying the foundation of a fundamentally new AI-based approach to the design of 6G systems. There are several areas in which ML and 6G can benefit from each other. First, while classical solutions for wireless systems are largely based on mathematical models and many simplifying assumptions, ML enables the compensation of model and algorithm deficits in a data-driven manner. Second, wireless networks are becoming increasingly important for supporting distributed large-scale ML applications. Hence, a co-designof ML applications with the 6G protocol stack is critical. Third, as wireless signals are increasingly used for perception and sensing, ML architectures for wireless sensing open up new exciting research areas. While there is significant potential for ML in wireless systems, many challenges exist. Wireless systems should operate with almost no access to labeled data and must be particularly robust to dynamically-changing network environments that vary at extremely short timescales. ML solutions should thus (i) generalize well to new scenarios with minimal supervision and (ii) deliver effective performance for mobile and severely resource-constrained devices. Addressing these challenges requires effectively combining wireless domain knowledge with a deep understanding of ML methods to develop high performance and practical solutions. Key ML domains that hold promise for application in wireless networks include uncertainty estimation, Bayesian learning, unsupervised and self-supervised learning, transfer learning, generative modeling, reinforcement learning, and graph representation learning. At the same time, communication and information theories also provide a new lens to design ML techniques. Already, communication-theoretic and ML research share approaches such as belief propagation and Kalman filtering. . Foundational theories of sampling, compression, quantization, coding, estimation, and detection are also relevant to develop new ML approaches that are better suited for dynamic wireless networks, and to enable large-scale ML over wireless communication systems.

Call for Papers

We will advertise a call for papers that solicits novel, unpublished research results on topics relevant to theworkshop. We will hold a dedicated spotlight session for individual presentations of a handful of spotlight accepted papers, as well as two poster sessions in order to cover the rest of the accepted submissions. In addition to the invited talks and spotlight/poster sessions, we will hold a panel discussion with panelists drawnfrom the invited talks. The panel session will be moderated by Prof. Monisha Ghosh from the University of Notre Dame.
